\begin{abstract}
We study Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) post-training of a 1.5B distilled
reasoning model under a single-A100 LoRA constraint, comparing three arms that vary
exactly one axis: training language (English vs Vietnamese-translated) and reward
function (with vs without a fastText-based language-consistency reward~$R_5$). On the
DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B base, English-only GRPO gains 7.5 percentage points (pp)
on AMC23 but loses 16.7 pp on AIME-2024 --- a benchmark-specific overfit at 50 steps.
\textbf{Adding the language-consistency reward $R_5$, even when it fires uniformly at
1.0 on English training data, recovers 13.3 pp on AIME-2024 and produces the highest
mean accuracy across four hard math metrics.} Vietnamese-translated training data
shows a milder version of the same regularization effect. Both findings hold on a
\$20 single-GPU LoRA budget, suggesting that auxiliary reward components and
out-of-domain training data offer cheap regularization mechanisms for sub-3B GRPO.
We release training/eval scripts, all checkpoints, and per-step reward traces.
\end{abstract}

% =====================================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
GRPO~\cite{deepseekmath_2024,deepseekR1_2025} drives recent gains in mathematical
reasoning for sub-3B models, but training corpora and reward functions are
overwhelmingly English. Prior work observes that GRPO at moderate scales causes
\emph{cross-lingual collapse}~\cite{xlingual_collapse_2025} and benchmark-specific
overfit~\cite{openRS_2025}. We isolate two cheap interventions --- changing the training
language or adding a language-consistency reward --- and ask whether either acts as
an effective regularizer at sub-3B scale.

\boxedclaim{%
\textbf{Main claim.} On DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B with a 50-step Open-RS Stage-1
GRPO recipe (LoRA $r{=}16$, single A100), pure English GRPO trades AMC23 gains for
AIME-2024 losses (+7.5 pp / $-$16.7 pp). Adding a fastText language-consistency reward
$R_5$ --- without changing training data --- recovers 13.3 pp on AIME-2024 and yields the
highest mean accuracy across four math benchmarks. Vietnamese-translated training
data shows the same regularization signature at smaller magnitude.
}

\paragraph{Why sub-3B and a single GPU?}
Sub-3B reasoning models are the deployment frontier for resource-constrained
applications. We deliberately target the budget ceiling at which most independent
researchers can reproduce GRPO results: a single A100 80GB on commodity cloud
($\sim$\$1.50/h). Open-RS~\cite{openRS_2025} reports 80\% AMC23 with 4$\times$A40
full-parameter training (192 GB total VRAM); we use LoRA $r{=}16$ as the natural
fallback at one-quarter VRAM and reach 57.5\%, a 22.5 pp gap that we document
explicitly.

\paragraph{What we find different from prior work.}
Open-RS does not vary training language or reward function.
\citet{xlingual_collapse_2025} report cross-lingual collapse at 3B+ but vary only test
language, not training language. M-Thinker~\cite{mthinker_2025} works at sub-3B but
focuses on Chinese-only multilingual training. Our three-arm comparison ---
\armA{} (EN), \armB{} (VI), \armC{} (EN+$R_5$) --- is, to our knowledge, the first
controlled study where exactly one axis (language or reward) is changed at a time
on a single base model and budget.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
(1) A reproducible single-GPU LoRA GRPO pipeline that achieves $+7.5$ pp on AMC23
over base (vs Open-RS reported $+17$ pp on 4$\times$A40); we document the gap honestly.
(2) Empirical evidence that English-only GRPO at 50 steps causes
\emph{benchmark-specific overfit}: gains concentrate on AMC23, losses concentrate
on AIME-2024.
(3) A surprising finding: a language-consistency reward $R_5$ --- even firing uniformly
at 1.0 on English data --- acts as an \emph{implicit regularizer}, recovering 13.3 pp
on AIME-2024 and improving mean accuracy. We hypothesize this is a reward-magnitude
effect on PPO clipping geometry, not a content effect.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{GRPO for small reasoning models.}
DeepSeekMath~\cite{deepseekmath_2024} introduced GRPO; DeepSeek-R1~\cite{deepseekR1_2025}
scaled it. Open-RS~\cite{openRS_2025} demonstrates 80\% AMC23 pass@1 on a 1.5B
distilled checkpoint (DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B) with 7K problems and 50 GRPO
steps. We adopt their hyperparameter recipe (RS2 setup: \texttt{accuracy}+\texttt{format}
rewards, weights $[1.0, 1.0]$, $G{=}6$, max\_completion 3584, learning rate $10^{-6}$)
verbatim for our \armA{} arm. Variance and length normalisation
variants---Dr.GRPO~\cite{drgrpo_2025} and DAPO~\cite{dapo_2025}---are orthogonal to
our axes and not used.

\paragraph{Cross-lingual reasoning.}
LinguaLIFT~\cite{lingualift_2024} and \citet{xreasoning_test_time_2025} explore
two-stage instruction tuning and test-time scaling at 7B+. MGSM~\cite{mgsm_2022} is
the standard multilingual math benchmark; OpenMathInstruct-2~\cite{openmathinstruct2_2024}
provides English-only training data. Cross-lingual
Collapse~\cite{xlingual_collapse_2025} is the closest concurrent work, observing
language drift during GRPO at 3B; our setting differs in three ways: (i) sub-3B with
a deliberate single-GPU LoRA constraint where prior baselines underperform their
reported numbers, (ii) controlled \emph{training-language} ablation rather than only
test-language variation, and (iii) explicit reward intervention as a treatment.
M-Thinker~\cite{mthinker_2025} is the closest scale-matched work but evaluates only
on Chinese; our setup admits direct extension to other languages.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Base model and training pipeline}
All three arms start from \texttt{deepseek-ai/DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B}~\cite{deepseekR1_2025},
the strongest publicly-available sub-2B distilled reasoning checkpoint. We \emph{do not}
use a separate SFT stage (matching Open-RS): training is GRPO directly from the
distilled base. Each cell is a single (arm, seed) pair: $\textsc{base}
\xrightarrow{\text{50-step GRPO}} \textsc{ckpt-50}$.

\subsection{Reward functions}
\label{sec:rewards}
All arms share two rewards (Open-RS RS2 setup):
\begin{itemize}\itemsep0pt
\item $R_1$ \textbf{correctness}: $\mathbb{1}[\text{Math-Verify}(\hat{y}, y^\star)]$,
extracting $\hat{y}$ from \texttt{<answer>} tag, then \texttt{\textbackslash boxed\{$\cdot$\}},
then last-number fallback.
\item $R_2$ \textbf{format}: $\mathbb{1}[\text{regex match
  \texttt{<think>...</think>...<answer>...</answer>}}]$, matching the
training system prompt template.
\end{itemize}
Arm \armC{} adds $R_5$ \textbf{lang-consistency}:
\[ R_5(\textbf{p}, \textbf{c}) = \begin{cases}
0 & |\textbf{c}| < 10 \text{ tokens (langID unreliable)} \\
\mathbb{1}[\text{fastText}(\textbf{c}) = \text{lang}(\textbf{p})] & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases} \]
fastText langID uses the public \texttt{lid.176.bin} model. Note: with English
training data, $R_5$ fires uniformly at 1.0 across nearly all generations
(model rarely code-switches on English prompts). We discuss the implication in §\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Three arms}
\begin{description}\itemsep0pt
\item[\armA{} (English-only):] training data = \texttt{knoveleng/open-rs}
(7K English problems, no SFT). Rewards: $R_1+R_2$.
\item[\armB{} (Vietnamese-translated):] training data =
\texttt{5CD-AI/Vietnamese-MetaMathQA-40K-gg-translated} (5{,}203 records extracted
from a 7K random subset, after filtering responses that lack the
``Đáp án là'' answer pattern). Rewards: $R_1+R_2$.
\item[\armC{} (English + $R_5$):] same training data as \armA{}, rewards
$R_1+R_2+R_5$ with $R_5$'s ``no\_penalty\_for\_en'' flag disabled so it can fire
on every prompt.
\end{description}

\subsection{Compute and hyperparameters}
1$\times$ NVIDIA A100-SXM4-80GB on Vast.ai. LoRA $r{=}16$,
$\alpha{=}32$, dropout 0.05, target = $\{q, k, v, o\}$\_proj. GRPO: $G{=}6$,
\texttt{max\_completion\_length}{=}3584, \texttt{temperature}{=}0.7,
$\beta_{\text{KL}}{=}0.04$, learning rate $10^{-6}$,
\texttt{cosine\_with\_min\_lr} (min\_lr\_rate=0.1, warmup 0.1), effective batch
96 prompts (per\_device\_batch 6 $\times$ grad\_accum 16), bf16, flash-attention 2,
gradient checkpointing. vLLM rollout uses \texttt{gpu\_memory\_utilization}=0.25
to leave 60 GB for training. We save at step 50 (Open-RS RS2 peak).

\subsection{Evaluation}
We use the Open-RS \texttt{lighteval} \texttt{MATH\_QUERY\_TEMPLATE} verbatim
(no system prompt, evaluation template embedded in the user message), with
greedy decoding ($T{=}0$), \texttt{max\_tokens}{=}8192, no early stop.
Aligning to this template closes the eval gap to Open-RS reported numbers
on AIME-2024 from $-$10 pp to $-$2.1 pp. Three benchmarks:
AMC23~\cite{amc23_dataset} (40 problems, pass@1),
MATH-500~\cite{lightman2024verify} (500, pass@1),
AIME-2024~\cite{aime2024_dataset} (30, pass@1 + maj@8 with seeds 42--49).

\subsection{Finding 1: English-only GRPO causes benchmark-specific overfit}
\label{sec:finding1}
\armA{} gains $+7.5$ pp on AMC23 but loses $-16.7$ pp on AIME-2024 pass@1 relative
to base (Table~\ref{tab:delta_vs_base}). This is consistent with the
``peak-then-degrade'' dynamics reported by Open-RS but observed here at smaller
absolute scale (50 steps from a distilled base). The model concentrates probability
mass on AMC23-style intermediate-difficulty patterns and loses the depth-of-reasoning
required for AIME's hardest problems. AIME maj@8 (which averages over 8 stochastic
samples) shows a smaller drop ($-3.3$ pp), suggesting capability is preserved in the
sample distribution but greedy decoding at \armA{}'s post-trained policy lands on
shallower trajectories.

\subsection{Finding 2: Vietnamese-translated training acts as a regularizer}
\label{sec:finding2}
\armB{} (VI training) shows the \emph{shape} of \armA{}'s degradation but at
smaller magnitude: $+2.5$ pp on AMC23, $-10.0$ pp on AIME pass@1, $0.0$ on AIME maj@8.
The model never overfits as aggressively because the training distribution
(Vietnamese MetaMathQA) is sufficiently far from the AMC23 evaluation distribution.
AIME maj@8 is fully preserved relative to base. We interpret this as
\emph{out-of-domain training acting as natural regularization}.

\subsection{Finding 3: Lang-consistency reward acts as implicit regularizer}
\label{sec:finding3}
\armC{} adds $R_5$ to the same English training data as \armA{}, with no other
change. The result reverses \armA{}'s catastrophic AIME degradation:
$+13.3$ pp pass@1 \emph{over \armA{}}, only $-3.4$ pp from base; AIME maj@8
\emph{exceeds} base by $+3.4$ pp. \armC{} also achieves the highest mean accuracy
across all four metrics (43.3\% vs base 42.4\%, vs \armA{}'s 39.1\%).

Critically, $R_5$ fires uniformly at 1.0 on \armA{}'s English training prompts
because the model rarely code-switches on English. The mean reward shifts from
$\mathbb{E}[R_1+R_2]\approx 0.2$ in \armA{} to $\mathbb{E}[R_1+R_2+R_5]\approx 1.2$
in \armC{}. Because GRPO computes per-group advantage as
$(r - \bar{r})/\text{std}(r)$, the mean shift cancels; only the variance changes.
However, total reward magnitude affects PPO clipping ratios, KL trade-offs, and
gradient norms, all of which are \emph{magnitude-sensitive} in the actual
implementation. Our hypothesis: the auxiliary reward acts as an implicit
regularizer by changing optimization geometry, not by injecting content signal.

\subsection{Bringing all three findings together}
Both \armB{} (different training distribution) and \armC{} (auxiliary reward
component) recover or exceed base performance on AIME-2024, while \armA{}
(vanilla EN-only GRPO) underperforms base by $-3.3$ pp on the 4-metric mean.
The two interventions act through different mechanisms --- distribution shift in
\armB{}, optimization-geometry shift in \armC{} --- but converge to the same
qualitative effect: \emph{prevention of benchmark-specific overfit at 50 steps}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why does \armC{} succeed despite $R_5$ being trivially satisfied?}
The standard view is that an auxiliary reward must inject useful content signal.
Our observation contradicts this: $R_5$ provides essentially no signal (uniform 1.0)
yet substantially improves the model. We attribute this to PPO clipping and KL
geometry. Higher mean reward $\bar{r}$ scales the advantage normalization, which
interacts with the trust-region width via the importance-sampling ratio
$r_t = \pi_\theta/\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$. If $\bar{r}$ is small, advantages are
near zero for in-distribution samples; gradient updates focus on the few standout
correct generations and overfit to their patterns. With higher $\bar{r}$,
the advantage-zero baseline is wider and updates are more uniformly spread.
This is testable: ablation with a constant additive bias on $R_1+R_2$ should
reproduce the regularization effect. We leave this for future work.

\paragraph{Implications for low-resource settings.}
Practitioners often assume that translating training data into a target language
($\armB$) is the main lever for cross-lingual transfer. Our results suggest a
cheaper alternative: keep the original training data, add a language-consistency
auxiliary reward. The R5 mechanism requires no translation infrastructure,
no additional training data, and yields better mean accuracy than translation
in our single experiment.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
\textbf{Single seed.} All numbers reported are from \texttt{seed=42}. The largest
effect sizes (\armA{}'s $-16.7$ pp on AIME, \armC{}'s $+13.3$ pp recovery)
substantially exceed plausible single-cell variance estimates ($\pm 5$ pp), but
mean differences ($\sim 1$ pp) are not statistically distinguishable. We commit
to a multi-seed extension prior to camera-ready.

\textbf{LoRA-only training.} Open-RS used full-parameter on 4$\times$A40; we use
LoRA $r{=}16$ on 1$\times$A100 to fit at the same effective batch size of 96.
This explains our 22.5 pp gap to Open-RS reported AMC23 (57.5\% vs 80.0\%).
Our findings about \emph{relative} arm comparisons may interact with LoRA's
limited update capacity in unknown ways.

\textbf{\armB{} dataset is not a faithful translation of \armA{}.} \armB{} uses
\texttt{5CD-AI/Vietnamese-MetaMathQA-40K-gg-translated}, a Google-translated
MetaMathQA, not a translation of the same Open-RS subset used in \armA{}.
Confounds between dataset content and language make our Finding 2 a
language-\emph{plus}-distribution effect rather than a clean language-only effect.

\textbf{Eval pipeline gap.} Even with the Open-RS \texttt{lighteval} template
verbatim, our base evaluation is $-12.9$ pp on AMC23 and $-23.4$ pp on MATH-500
relative to Open-RS reported. The gap is unexplained; we report all numbers vs
\emph{our} base rather than Open-RS-reported numbers.

\textbf{\texttt{maj@4} on AMC23 is zero across all conditions} due to a
yet-undiagnosed bug in our majority-vote sampling. We report \texttt{pass@1} on
AMC23 and \texttt{pass@1}/\texttt{maj@8} on AIME-2024.

\textbf{Single base model and scale.} Results are on
DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B only. Generalization to Qwen2.5-Instruct or 3B+ untested.

\textbf{No multilingual evaluation yet.} The paper's full cross-lingual claim
requires MGSM and MSVAMP eval, currently in progress. The present manuscript
focuses on the English benchmark behavior under the three training arms.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We compared three GRPO arms at sub-3B scale on a single-GPU LoRA budget: pure
English (\armA{}), Vietnamese-translated (\armB{}), and English plus a
fastText-based language-consistency reward (\armC{}). \armA{} exhibits
benchmark-specific overfit ($+7.5$ AMC23 / $-16.7$ AIME-2024). \armB{} and
\armC{} both prevent this overfit, with \armC{} achieving the highest mean
accuracy. Notably, \armC{}'s $R_5$ provides no content signal on English data
(it fires at 1.0 nearly everywhere) yet still produces substantial regularization,
suggesting that the regularization mechanism is a reward-magnitude effect on PPO
optimization geometry rather than a learned content effect. We release all code,
configs, LoRA adapters, and per-step reward traces.
