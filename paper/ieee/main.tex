%% =================== STRUCTURED ABSTRACT =============================
\begin{abstract}
\noindent\textbf{Background.} Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO)
drives recent advances in mathematical reasoning for sub-3B language models,
but recent reproducibility analysis~\cite{sober_look_2025} shows that
small-model RL gains may not survive multi-seed evaluation. The role of
training-language choice and auxiliary reward components remains
under-studied at the deployment-relevant single-GPU LoRA scale.

\noindent\textbf{Methods.} On the DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B base, we run
a controlled multi-seed GRPO study under a single A100~80\,GB LoRA constraint
(rank $r{=}16$). Three primary arms vary exactly one axis:
\armA{} (English training data, accuracy and format rewards),
\armB{} (Vietnamese-translated training data, identical rewards), and
\armC{} (English training data, with an additional fastText-based
language-consistency reward~$\R{5}$). Each arm is trained with three random
seeds. A three-seed constant-bias ablation \armD{} tests whether the
auxiliary-reward effect reduces to reward magnitude. Models are evaluated on
AMC-23, MATH-500, AIME-2024, and 10-language MGSM.

\noindent\textbf{Results.} We report three orthogonal findings.
\textbf{(i) Positive:} on AIME-2024 maj@8, the hardest benchmark in our
suite, \armC{} achieves the highest mean accuracy ($37.8 \pm 1.9$\%) and the
only positive effect over the untrained base ($+4.4$\,pp), robust across
three seeds. \textbf{(ii) Methodological:} \armA{} exhibits
$\sigma = 11.3$\,pp seed variance on AMC-23 and consistently degrades
AIME-2024 pass@1 (mean $-12.2$\,pp across 3/3 seeds), confirming that
single-seed claims at sub-3B + LoRA scale are unreliable.
\textbf{(iii) Negative:} on the MGSM 10-language benchmark, all four
training conditions converge to within $\pm 0.5$\,pp of the untrained base
mean, indicating that training-language choice does not produce cross-lingual
transfer at this scale. The three-seed \armD{} ablation \emph{fails to}
reproduce \armC{}'s effect on AIME-2024 maj@8 ($\armC{}-\armD{} = +5.58$\,pp,
$95\%$ bootstrap CI $[+1.13, +11.10]$, excluding $0$), refuting a pure
reward-magnitude interpretation.

\noindent\textbf{Conclusion.} The auxiliary language-consistency reward
$\R{5}$ improves the hardest-benchmark capability while preserving
multilingual performance, but the mechanism is not purely a reward-magnitude
perturbation. Multi-seed evaluation reveals that vanilla English GRPO at
sub-3B + LoRA scale is unreliable; reproducibility studies are essential
before scaling claims. We release all code, configurations, LoRA adapters,
evaluation JSONs, and per-step reward traces to support replication.
\end{abstract}

%% =================== I. INTRODUCTION =================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\IEEEPARstart{R}{einforcement} learning from verifier signals has become the
dominant paradigm for improving mathematical reasoning in mid-sized language
models. \emph{Group Relative Policy Optimization} (GRPO)~\cite{deepseekmath_2024,
deepseekR1_2025} is the workhorse of this paradigm. By eliciting a group of
candidate completions per prompt and assigning advantages relative to the group
mean, GRPO removes the need for a learned value head while preserving on-policy
PPO-style updates~\cite{schulman2017ppo}. Recent work on small reasoning models
(Open-RS~\cite{openRS_2025}) has shown that GRPO can lift a 1.5\,B distilled
checkpoint to 80\,\% pass@1 on the AMC-23 competition benchmark with only 50
optimization steps and 7\,K training problems on four NVIDIA A40 GPUs.

Two structural assumptions, however, are baked into nearly every published
GRPO recipe:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Monolingual English training corpus}. The training problems and
their reference solutions are in English; multilingual generalization is
assumed rather than measured.
\item \emph{Reward sum dominated by accuracy and format}. Auxiliary rewards
that do not directly score correctness are rarely included; when they are,
their effect is interpreted as a content signal.
\end{enumerate}
The downstream behaviour of GRPO when these assumptions are perturbed has
received limited systematic attention at the sub-3\,B scale that is most
relevant to deployed systems and to independent researchers operating on
commodity cloud budgets.

\subsection{Research questions}
This article addresses two questions, both targeted at the single-GPU
sub-3\,B regime:
\begin{itemize}
\item[\textbf{Q1.}] How does the choice of training language (English vs.
Vietnamese-translated, holding the reward set fixed) affect downstream
mathematical reasoning, and is the effect uniform across benchmarks of
varying difficulty?
\item[\textbf{Q2.}] When an auxiliary reward component is added that fires
nearly uniformly at $1.0$ across training generations -- so it carries
\emph{no} content signal -- does its presence still affect the trained
policy? If so, through what mechanism?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Contributions}
We make four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Multi-seed empirical evidence (3 seeds $\times$ 4 arms) that
single-seed GRPO results at sub-3\,B + LoRA scale are unreliable: vanilla
English GRPO exhibits $\sigma{=}11.3$\,pp variance on AMC-23
(Section~\ref{sec:finding1}).
\item Documentation of \armC{}'s positive effect on the hardest benchmark
in our suite: $+4.4 \pm 1.9$\,pp on AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8} over the
untrained base, robust across three seeds
(Section~\ref{sec:finding3}).
\item A three-seed constant-bias ablation \armD{} that \emph{fails} to
reproduce \armC{}'s AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8} result
($\armC{}{-}\armD{}{=}{+}5.58$\,pp, $95\%$ bootstrap CI
$[+1.13, +11.10]$, excluding $0$), demonstrating that $\R{5}$'s
contribution is content-specific rather than a pure reward-magnitude
perturbation (Section~\ref{sec:mechanism}).
\item A 10-language MGSM evaluation showing that the GRPO recipe at this
scale \emph{preserves but does not improve} multilingual capability
regardless of training-language choice
(Section~\ref{sec:results}).
\end{enumerate}

\boxedclaim{%
\textbf{Main empirical claim.} Multi-seed evaluation reveals three
orthogonal patterns. \textbf{(i)}~The language-consistency reward arm
\armC{} achieves the highest mean AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8}
($+4.4 \pm 1.9$\,pp over base across three seeds). \textbf{(ii)}~Vanilla
English GRPO \armA{} exhibits $\sigma{=}11.3$\,pp seed variance on
AMC-23 and consistently degrades AIME-2024 \texttt{pass@1} by
$-12.2 \pm 7.7$\,pp. \textbf{(iii)}~On 10-language MGSM, all training
conditions converge to within $\pm 0.5$\,pp of the untrained base. A
three-seed constant-bias control \armD{} \emph{fails} to reproduce
\armC{}'s effect on AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8}
($\armC{}{-}\armD{}{=}{+}5.58$\,pp, $95\%$ bootstrap CI
$[+1.13, +11.10]$), refuting a pure reward-magnitude explanation.
}

%% =================== II. RELATED WORK ================================
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{GRPO and small reasoning models}
DeepSeekMath~\cite{deepseekmath_2024} introduced GRPO, replacing PPO's learned
value head with a group-relative baseline. DeepSeek-R1~\cite{deepseekR1_2025}
scaled this technique to produce strong reasoning checkpoints whose distilled
variants -- including the 1.5\,B model used here -- have become standard
sub-3\,B baselines. Open-RS~\cite{openRS_2025} explored what hyperparameter
recipes are needed to lift a distilled 1.5\,B to 80\,\% on AMC-23; we adopt
their RS2 setup verbatim for our \armA{} arm. Variance-reduced and
length-normalized variants (Dr.GRPO~\cite{drgrpo_2025}, DAPO~\cite{dapo_2025})
are orthogonal to the axes we vary.

\subsection{Cross-lingual mathematical reasoning}
Multilingual mathematical reasoning has been studied primarily at 7\,B and
above. LinguaLIFT~\cite{lingualift_2024} proposes a two-stage instruction
tuning framework. MAPO~\cite{mapo_2024} and
MathOctopus~\cite{mathoctopus_2024} fine-tune open models for multilingual
mathematical reasoning at 7\,B scale. Crosslingual reasoning under
test-time scaling~\cite{xreasoning_test_time_2025} examines inference-time
multilinguality. The multilingual GSM benchmark~\cite{mgsm_2022} is the
de-facto evaluation harness for language transfer; OpenMathInstruct-2~%
\cite{openmathinstruct2_2024} provides English-only training data.
Cross-lingual collapse~\cite{xlingual_collapse_2025} is the closest concurrent
work, observing language drift during GRPO at $\geq 3$\,B scale; our setting
differs in three respects: (i) sub-3\,B with a deliberate single-GPU LoRA
budget at which prior baselines underperform their reported numbers,
(ii) controlled \emph{training-language} ablation in addition to
test-language variation, and (iii) explicit reward intervention as a
treatment. M-Thinker~\cite{mthinker_2025} is the closest scale-matched work
but evaluates only on Chinese; our setup admits direct extension to other
languages without methodological change.

\subsection{Auxiliary rewards and reward shaping}
Reward shaping is well-studied in classical RL.
Ng~et~al.~\cite{ng1999policy} prove that potential-based shaping preserves
the optimal policy, while Skalse~et~al.~\cite{skalse2022rewardhacking}
characterize how mis-specified auxiliary rewards can induce reward
hacking. Zheng~et~al.~\cite{zheng_intrinsic_2018} study learned intrinsic
rewards in policy gradients and use constant-bias baselines that are
conceptually analogous to our \armD{} ablation. In language-model fine-tuning, auxiliary reward
components have been used to steer models toward desired stylistic or
safety properties; the conventional interpretation is that they inject
content-level signal into the policy gradient. We are not aware of prior
work that examines what happens when an auxiliary reward fires nearly
uniformly across training samples and therefore carries minimal content
information. Our \armC{} arm provides a natural setting to test this
regime, and our hypothesis (Section~\ref{sec:mechanism}) makes a
falsifiable prediction about why such a ``trivially-satisfied'' auxiliary
reward can still substantially affect the trained policy.

%% =================== III. PRELIMINARIES ==============================
\section{Preliminaries: GRPO and PPO Clipping Geometry}
\label{sec:preliminaries}

We restate the relevant pieces of the GRPO objective to fix notation for the
mechanistic discussion in Section~\ref{sec:mechanism}.

\subsection{GRPO objective}
Let $\pi_\theta$ denote the policy, $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ the
behaviour policy at the start of the optimization step, and $\mathbf{p}$ a
prompt sampled from the training distribution. For each prompt, GRPO samples
a group of $G$ completions
$\{\mathbf{c}^{(1)},\ldots,\mathbf{c}^{(G)}\}$ and computes a scalar reward
$R(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{c}^{(g)})\in\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ per completion. The
group-relative advantage is
\begin{equation}
A^{(g)} = \frac{R(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{c}^{(g)}) - \bar{R}_{\mathbf{p}}}%
              {\sigma_{R,\mathbf{p}} + \varepsilon},
\label{eq:advantage}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{R}_{\mathbf{p}} = \tfrac{1}{G}\sum_g R(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{c}^{(g)})$
and $\sigma_{R,\mathbf{p}}$ is the within-group standard deviation. Per-token
importance ratios
\begin{equation}
\rho_t^{(g)} = \frac{\pi_\theta(c^{(g)}_t \mid \mathbf{p}, \mathbf{c}^{(g)}_{<t})}%
                      {\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(c^{(g)}_t \mid \mathbf{p}, \mathbf{c}^{(g)}_{<t})}
\end{equation}
are clipped via the standard PPO-style trust region, and the GRPO objective is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{GRPO}}(\theta) =
\E_{\mathbf{p}}\!\left[\sum_g \sum_t \min\!\left(\rho_t^{(g)} A^{(g)},\;
                            \mathrm{clip}(\rho_t^{(g)},1-\epsilon,1+\epsilon)\,
                            A^{(g)}\right)\right]
- \beta_{\text{KL}}\,\KL(\pi_\theta\,\|\,\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}).
\label{eq:grpo}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Reward decomposition with multiple components}
With multiple reward components $\R{1},\R{2},\ldots,\R{K}$ and weights
$w_k$, the per-completion reward is
$R(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{c}) = \sum_k w_k \R{k}(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{c})$.
A useful identity for our discussion: for any constant $b\in\mathbb{R}$,
\begin{equation}
A^{(g)}\!\left[R + b\right]
= \frac{(R + b) - (\bar{R}+b)}{\sigma_R + \varepsilon}
= A^{(g)}\!\left[R\right],
\label{eq:advantage-invariance}
\end{equation}
i.e., adding a constant offset to every completion's reward has no effect on
the advantage. This is the textbook reason ``trivial'' reward components are
believed to be inert. The point of Section~\ref{sec:mechanism} is that
\eqref{eq:advantage-invariance} ignores the rest of the optimization
machinery -- in particular the clipping ratio $\rho_t^{(g)}$ and the KL
penalty -- which depend on \emph{realized rewards}, not advantages, and are
therefore \emph{not} invariant to additive offsets.

%% =================== IV. METHOD ======================================
\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Base model and training pipeline}
All three arms start from the publicly-released
\path{deepseek-ai/DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B}
checkpoint~\cite{deepseekR1_2025}. We do \emph{not} use a separate
supervised fine-tuning stage (matching Open-RS): training is GRPO directly
from the distilled \base{} for $50$ steps, producing one LoRA adapter per
(arm, seed) cell.

\subsection{Reward functions}
\label{sec:rewards}
All arms use two rewards from the Open-RS RS2 setup:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep0pt
\item $\R{1}$ \emph{correctness}: indicator of
Math-Verify~\cite{mathverify_2024} equivalence between the extracted
answer $\hat{y}$ and the gold $y^\star$. Extraction priority:
\texttt{<answer>} tag, then \texttt{\textbackslash boxed\{\}}, then
last-number fallback.
\item $\R{2}$ \emph{format}: indicator that the completion matches a regular
expression requiring both a \texttt{<think>...</think>} block and an
\texttt{<answer>...</answer>} block, in that order; matches the training
system prompt template.
\end{itemize}
Arm \armC{} adds the language-consistency reward
$\R{5}$, computed with the fastText~\cite{joulin2017fasttext}
language identifier:
\begin{equation}
\R{5}(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{c}) =
\begin{cases}
0 & \text{if } |\mathbf{c}| < 10,\\[2pt]
\mathbf{1}\!\left[\mathrm{lang}(\mathbf{c}) = \mathrm{lang}(\mathbf{p})\right]
                & \text{otherwise,}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:r5}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{lang}(\cdot)$ is the fastText \texttt{lid.176.bin} language
identifier and the $|\mathbf{c}|<10$ guard accounts for fastText's
unreliability on very short strings. Crucially, when training data is
English the model rarely code-switches; thus $\R{5}\approx 1$ for almost
every training generation, and $\R{5}$ provides effectively zero
content-discrimination signal in this regime.

\subsection{Three arms}
\begin{itemize}\itemsep0pt
\item[\armA{} -- English-only:] training data \texttt{knoveleng/open-rs}
(7\,K English problems); rewards $\R{1}+\R{2}$.
\item[\armB{} -- Vietnamese-translated:] training data
\path{5CD-AI/Vietnamese-MetaMathQA-40K-gg-translated} (5\,203 records
extracted from a 7\,K random subset by retaining only those whose response
contains the Vietnamese answer marker ``\DJ\'ap \'an l\`a''); rewards
$\R{1}+\R{2}$.
\item[\armC{} -- English with $\R{5}$:] same training data as \armA{};
rewards $\R{1}+\R{2}+\R{5}$ with $\R{5}$'s ``no\_penalty\_for\_en'' flag
disabled so it can fire on every prompt regardless of input language.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Compute and hyperparameters}
Hardware: $1\times$ NVIDIA A100-SXM4-80\,GB on commodity cloud
infrastructure. LoRA~\cite{hu2022lora}: $r{=}16$, $\alpha{=}32$, dropout
$0.05$, \texttt{bias=none}, target modules
$\{q,k,v,o\}\_\mathrm{proj}$. GRPO: $G{=}6$ rollouts per prompt,
\texttt{max\_completion\_length}$=3584$ tokens, \texttt{temperature}$=0.7$,
$\beta_{\text{KL}}=0.04$, learning rate $10^{-6}$, scheduler
\texttt{cosine\_with\_min\_lr} with \texttt{min\_lr\_rate}$=0.1$ and
\texttt{warmup\_ratio}$=0.1$. Effective batch is 96 prompts (per-device
batch 6 $\times$ gradient accumulation 16), \texttt{bf16},
flash-attention~2, gradient checkpointing
(\texttt{use\_reentrant=False}). Training uses TRL~\cite{trl_2025}
0.15.2 with in-process vLLM~\cite{kwon2023vllm} 0.7.2 rollout
(\texttt{gpu\_memory\_utilization}$=0.25$, leaving $\sim 60$\,GB for the
training pass). We save at step 50 (Open-RS RS2 peak).

\subsection{Evaluation}
We use the Open-RS \texttt{lighteval} \texttt{MATH\_QUERY\_TEMPLATE}
verbatim (no system prompt; the evaluation template is embedded in the
user message), with greedy decoding ($T{=}0$), \texttt{max\_tokens}$=8192$,
and no early stop tokens. Aligning to this template closes the AIME-2024
gap to Open-RS reported numbers from $-10$\,pp to $-2.1$\,pp. Three
benchmarks are used: AMC-23~\cite{amc23_dataset} (40 problems, pass@1),
MATH-500~\cite{lightman2024verify} (500 problems, pass@1), and
AIME-2024~\cite{aime2024_dataset} (30 problems, pass@1 plus maj@8 with
seeds 42--49 to cover the standard 8-sample majority-vote evaluation).

\subsection{Reproducibility}
Code, configuration files, LoRA adapters, evaluation JSONs (with full
\texttt{responses[]} arrays), and per-step training logs are publicly
archived. Versioned dependencies: Python 3.12.3,
\texttt{torch==2.5.1+cu124}, \texttt{transformers==4.49.0},
\texttt{trl==0.15.2}, \texttt{vllm==0.7.2}, \texttt{accelerate==1.2.1},
\texttt{peft==0.14.0}, \texttt{datasets==4.8.5},
\texttt{flash\_attn==2.7.2.post1}, \texttt{sympy==1.13.1},
\texttt{math\_verify==0.9.0}, \texttt{fasttext-wheel==0.9.2}.

%% =================== V. EXPERIMENTAL SETUP ==========================
\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Training cells}
A \emph{cell} is a single (arm, seed) pair yielding one LoRA adapter at
step~50. We report three random seeds (\texttt{42, 123, 7}) for each of
the four arms (\armA{}, \armB{}, \armC{}, and the constant-bias ablation
\armD{}). Each cell shares the same Open-RS Stage-1 hyperparameters
($G{=}6$, $T{=}0.7$ for rollout, max\_completion 3584, learning rate
$10^{-6}$, KL coefficient $\beta{=}0.04$, cosine LR with min\_lr\_rate
0.1 and warmup 0.1, effective batch 96 prompts on $1\times$A100 with LoRA
$r{=}16$); the only across-cell variations are the random seed and the
arm-specific reward composition (\armA{}: $R_1{+}R_2$; \armB{}: $R_1{+}R_2$
on the Vietnamese-translated dataset; \armC{}: $R_1{+}R_2{+}R_5$;
\armD{}: $R_1{+}R_2{+}R_{\text{const}}$, $R_{\text{const}}{\equiv}1.0$).

\subsection{Evaluation seeds}
Pass@1 evaluations are deterministic at $T{=}0$. AIME-2024 maj@8 uses
$T{=}0.7$, \texttt{top\_p}$=0.95$, with seeds $42,43,\ldots,49$ to draw the
8 stochastic samples per problem. AMC-23 maj@4 uses the same stochastic
sampling with seeds $42,43,44,45$ (the post-fix \texttt{maj@4} pipeline; see
\S\ref{sec:limitations} ``\texttt{maj@4} bug found and fixed'').

\subsection{Statistical reporting}
Per-arm point estimates are reported as mean $\pm$ standard deviation
across the three seeds (Tables~\ref{tab:main} and~\ref{tab:delta}).
For the headline AIME-2024 maj@8 comparisons, we additionally report
$95\%$ subject-bootstrap confidence intervals over seed-level means
($n{=}10{,}000$ resamples, fixed RNG seed): \armC{}$-\base{}$ has
CI $[+3.3, +6.7]$ and \armC{}$-\armD{}$ has CI $[+1.13, +11.10]$, both
excluding $0$ at the $5\%$ level. Bootstrap implementation is in
\path{scripts/w110_bootstrap_ci.py} and \path{scripts/w21_verify_paper_claims.py};
the verification script re-derives every numerical claim in the manuscript
from the per-seed JSONs in \path{results/eval/}.

\subsection{Finding 1: English-only GRPO causes benchmark-specific overfit}
\label{sec:finding1}
Averaged over three seeds, \armA{} gains $+6.7 \pm 11.3$\,pp on AMC-23 and
loses $-12.2 \pm 7.7$\,pp on AIME-2024 \texttt{pass@1} relative to the
untrained base (Table~\ref{tab:delta}). The AMC-23 lift is not
statistically distinguishable from zero given the variance, but the
AIME-2024 \texttt{pass@1} degradation is robust: $3/3$ seeds fall below
base. AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8} (which averages over eight stochastic
samples) shows a smaller mean drop ($-1.1 \pm 6.9$\,pp), suggesting that
the underlying capability is partially preserved in the sample
distribution but greedy decoding under \armA{}'s policy lands on
shallower trajectories more frequently. This is consistent with the
``peak-then-degrade'' dynamics reported by Open-RS, here observed at
smaller absolute scale (50 steps from a distilled base on a single GPU).

\subsection{Finding 2: Both \armB{} and \armC{} reduce seed variance}
\label{sec:finding2}
Across three seeds, \armA{} exhibits $\sigma{=}11.3$\,pp on AMC-23 and
$\sigma{=}6.9$\,pp on AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8}. Both \armB{} (Vietnamese
training) and \armC{} (English with $\R{5}$) reduce this variance
substantially: $\sigma{=}5.2$\,pp and $6.6$\,pp respectively on AMC-23,
and $\sigma{=}1.9$\,pp for both on AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8}. The mean
lifts on AMC-23 are statistically indistinguishable across arms
($56.7$, $56.7$, $57.5$\,\%); the differentiation between arms appears
in \emph{reproducibility} rather than \emph{mean performance}. We
interpret this as both interventions acting as implicit regularizers
that shrink the policy-update variance across seeds.

\subsection{Finding 3: Lang-consistency reward is best on AIME-2024
\texttt{maj@8}}
\label{sec:finding3}
\armC{} adds $\R{5}$ to the same English training data as \armA{}, with
no other change. Across three seeds, \armC{} achieves the highest mean
AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8} ($37.8 \pm 1.9$\,\%) and is the only arm with a
positive $\Delta$ over the untrained base ($+4.4$\,pp;
Fig.~\ref{fig:effect_sizes}). The effect is robust: all three seeds
produce $36.7$\,\% or higher.

The per-completion reward decomposes as
\begin{align*}
\armA{}: \quad & R = \R{1} + \R{2}, \quad \E[R]\approx 0.2, \\
\armC{}: \quad & R = \R{1} + \R{2} + \R{5}, \quad \E[R]\approx 1.2,
\end{align*}
i.e., the $\R{5}$ component shifts the reward distribution upward by
approximately a constant ($\R{5}$ fires near-uniformly at $1.0$ on
English training data). By the advantage-invariance
identity~\eqref{eq:advantage-invariance}, this offset is invisible to the
within-group advantage $A^{(g)}$. The empirical effect is therefore not
naturally explained by the standard advantage-only view of policy
gradient optimization; we test the natural ``magnitude-only'' alternative
via the constant-bias ablation \armD{} (Section~\ref{sec:mechanism}).

\subsection{Synthesis across findings}
\label{sec:synthesis}
Both \armB{} (different training distribution) and \armC{} (auxiliary
reward component) reduce seed variance relative to \armA{} on every
metric, while \armC{} additionally produces the only positive
$\Delta$ over base on AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8} ($+4.4 \pm 1.9$\,pp).
The two interventions act through different proximal mechanisms --
distribution shift in \armB{}, an auxiliary reward in \armC{} -- but
converge to the same qualitative effect: \emph{improved
reproducibility under a fixed compute budget at 50 steps}. The
content-specific component of \armC{}'s effect, isolated via the
\armD{} ablation in Section~\ref{sec:mechanism}, is what differentiates
\armC{} from \armB{} on the hardest benchmark.

%% =================== VII. MECHANISM ==================================
\section{Discussion: Why Does \armC{} Succeed?}
\label{sec:mechanism}

The standard interpretation of an auxiliary reward component is that it
injects useful content signal into the policy gradient. Our empirical
setting initially appears to contradict this view: $\R{5}$ fires
near-uniformly at $1.0$ on \armC{}'s English training data, providing
essentially no content-discrimination signal -- yet \armC{} produces
measurable downstream effects. Two natural hypotheses can explain this
anomaly: (H1)~a pure reward-magnitude effect on the rest of the GRPO
optimization machinery despite the advantage-invariance
identity~\eqref{eq:advantage-invariance}; or (H2)~a content-specific
effect carried by the rare training generations where $\R{5}\to 0$. We
state H1 precisely below and then refute it via the constant-bias
ablation \armD{}.

\subsection{Hypothesis H1: the PPO-clipping geometry hypothesis}
The advantage-invariance identity \eqref{eq:advantage-invariance} shows that
an additive offset is invisible to $A^{(g)}$. However, the GRPO loss
\eqref{eq:grpo} contains two additional terms whose sensitivity to absolute
reward magnitude is non-trivial:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Importance-sampling ratio clipping.} Each per-token ratio
$\rho_t^{(g)}$ is clipped to $[1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon]$. When the per-step
reward magnitude is small relative to the implicit gradient noise (as in
\armA{} where $\E[R]\approx 0.21$), the few completions with non-zero
advantages produce large per-token gradients, increasing the probability of
hitting the clip boundary. Clip activations introduce gradient
discontinuities and bias the update toward the few high-advantage trajectories.
With a higher reward floor (as in \armC{}), the same advantages produce
smaller relative log-probability swings, reducing clip activation rates and
producing softer, more spatially-distributed updates.
\item \textbf{KL penalty interaction.} The $\beta_{\text{KL}}$ term in
\eqref{eq:grpo} penalizes deviation of $\pi_\theta$ from
$\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ uniformly across tokens. When advantages are
sparse and concentrated, the KL penalty pulls back the few large updates,
producing oscillatory training. When advantages are denser (because the
reward floor is higher, so more completions cross the gradient-active
threshold), the KL penalty distributes more uniformly and damps the
oscillation.
\end{enumerate}
Both effects predict that an additive constant added to every reward
should \emph{produce the same regularization signature as $\R{5}$},
regardless of whether $\R{5}$ correlates with any property of the
completion. This is a falsifiable prediction that we operationalize
via the constant-bias ablation \armD{} below.

\subsection{Ablation \armD{} (constant-bias reward) refutes H1}
We define a fourth arm \armD{} that uses
\begin{equation*}
R_{\armD{}}(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{c}) = \R{1}+\R{2}+\R{\text{const}}, \quad
\R{\text{const}} \equiv 1.0,
\end{equation*}
i.e., the language-consistency reward replaced by a function that
deterministically returns $1.0$ for every input. Under H1, \armD{}
should reproduce \armC{}'s regularization signature on AIME-2024
\texttt{maj@8} within seed variance. Three-seed results
(\texttt{42, 123, 7}): \armD{} achieves AMC-23 \texttt{maj@4}
$=70.0\pm 2.5$\,\%, MATH-500 $=61.2\pm 0.9$\,\%, AIME-2024
\texttt{pass@1} $=24.4\pm 1.9$\,\%, and AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8}
$=32.2\pm 5.1$\,\% (below \armC{}'s $37.8\pm 1.9$\,\%;
$\armC{}-\armD{} = +5.58$\,pp, $95\%$ bootstrap CI $[+1.13, +11.10]$
excluding $0$ over $10{,}000$ subject-bootstrap resamples). \armD{}
itself is not distinguishable from base on AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8}
($\Delta=-1.08\pm 5.07$\,pp; CI includes $0$).

\textbf{H1 is refuted.} $\R{5}$'s contribution is content-specific, not
a pure reward-magnitude perturbation. We therefore favour H2 (a content
effect carried by the rare $\R{5}\to 0$ generations); a clean
attribution would require gradient-tracing on individual training
completions, which we leave to future work. The implementation,
configuration, and unit tests for \armD{} are included in the released
codebase.

\subsection{Implications for low-resource settings}
A common assumption in cross-lingual RL fine-tuning is that translating
training data into the target language is the principal lever for
transfer. Our results suggest a more nuanced picture for English-data-rich
settings where the target is robustness rather than language coverage:
adding a language-consistency auxiliary reward to the same English data
improves the hardest-benchmark capability while preserving multilingual
performance, but the mechanism is content-specific (\armD{} refutes a
pure-magnitude shortcut). Practitioners considering this lever should
expect content-dependent gains rather than a free constant-bias
regularizer.

%% =================== VIII. LIMITATIONS ===============================
\section{Limitations and Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:limitations}

\textbf{Three-seed sample (all four arms).} Each of \armA{}, \armB{},
\armC{}, and the constant-bias ablation \armD{} reports three random seeds
(\texttt{42, 123, 7}). The headline \armC{} effect on AIME-2024 maj@8
($+4.4\pm 1.9$\,pp over base; $95\%$ bootstrap CI $[+3.3, +6.7]$) and the
\armC{}-vs-\armD{} contrast ($+5.58$\,pp, $95\%$ bootstrap CI
$[+1.13, +11.10]$) are both statistically distinguishable from zero under
subject bootstrap with $10{,}000$ resamples. Bootstrap CIs are wide due to
small $n$; $n=3$ is the standard for sub-3\,B GRPO replication studies but a
larger seed sample would tighten the intervals further.

\textbf{LoRA fallback versus full-parameter training.} Open-RS used
full-parameter training on 4$\times$A40 (192\,GB total VRAM). We use LoRA
$r{=}16$ on 1$\times$A100 (80\,GB) at the same effective batch size of 96
prompts. Findings about \emph{relative} arm comparisons may interact with
LoRA's restricted update subspace in unknown ways. The apparent 22.5\,pp
gap to Open-RS-reported $80$\,\% AMC-23 dissolves under like-for-like
metric comparison: \texttt{maj@4} on Open-RS's own public RS2 checkpoint
gives $75.0$\,\% in our pipeline, only $5$\,pp below Open-RS's headline
$80$\,\% (see ``Evaluation pipeline gap'' below).

\textbf{\armB{} dataset is not a faithful translation of \armA{}.} Arm
\armB{} uses
\path{5CD-AI/Vietnamese-MetaMathQA-40K-gg-translated} -- a Google-translated
MetaMathQA -- not a translation of the same Open-RS subset used in \armA{}.
Confounds between dataset content and language make Finding~2 a
language-\emph{plus}-distribution effect rather than a clean language-only
effect. A clean translation of the open-rs subset is required to fully
isolate the language axis.

\textbf{Evaluation pipeline gap explained by metric mismatch.} Open-RS's
reported $80$\,\% AMC-23 headline for the RS2 checkpoint is majority-vote
(\texttt{maj@k}), not greedy \texttt{pass@1}. Evaluating the public
\texttt{knoveleng/Open-RS2} checkpoint with our pipeline gives
\texttt{pass@1}$=52.5$\,\% (within sampling variance of our paper's $57.5$\,\%
LoRA best arm) and \texttt{maj@4}$=75.0$\,\% (only $5$\,pp below Open-RS's
$80$\,\% headline). The base \texttt{DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B} gives
\texttt{pass@1}$=50.0$\,\% (matching our paper's $50.0$\,\% base claim) and
\texttt{maj@4}$=70.0$\,\% (vs Open-RS-reported $62.9$\,\%, $+7$\,pp above).
The original $12.9$\,pp gap was a metric-mismatch artifact, not a pipeline bug.

\textbf{\texttt{maj@4} on AMC-23 -- bug found and fixed.} The pre-revision
\texttt{maj@4}$=0$ numbers across all arms were caused by two bugs:
(i)~\texttt{eval.yaml} omitted a \texttt{temperature\_maj} field, causing
all four maj samples to be deterministic and identical;
(ii)~\texttt{extract\_prediction()} did not strip whitespace, causing valid
answers to fail string-matching. Both fixes are in the current codebase;
the post-fix \texttt{maj@4} numbers are reported in Table~\ref{tab:main}.

\textbf{Single base model and scale.} Results are on
DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B only. Generalization to Qwen2.5-Instruct or
the 3\,B+ regime is untested in this manuscript.

\textbf{MGSM evaluation has limited seed coverage.} The 10-language
MGSM evaluation (Table~\ref{tab:mgsm}) reports a single seed
(\texttt{42}) for the untrained base and \armD{}, and the mean of two
seeds (\texttt{123}, \texttt{7}) for \armA{}, \armB{}, \armC{} -- in
contrast to the three-seed AMC-23/MATH-500/AIME-2024 suite. The
$\pm 0.5$\,pp convergence observed across arms is within the range of
single-run variance on multilingual benchmarks; a full three-seed MGSM
evaluation is left to future work due to the additional $\approx 6$
A100-hours required per run. MSVAMP evaluation is omitted in this
manuscript and deferred to a follow-on submission.

%% =================== IX. CONCLUSION ==================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We compared three GRPO arms at sub-3\,B scale on a single-GPU LoRA
budget: pure English (\armA{}), Vietnamese-translated (\armB{}), and
English with an additional fastText-based language-consistency reward
(\armC{}). Multi-seed evaluation reveals three orthogonal findings.
\textbf{(i)~Positive.} \armC{} achieves the highest mean AIME-2024
\texttt{maj@8} ($37.8 \pm 1.9$\,\%, $+4.4$\,pp over the untrained base)
and is the only arm with a positive $\Delta$ on the hardest benchmark,
robust across three seeds.
\textbf{(ii)~Methodological.} \armA{} exhibits seed variance
($\sigma{=}11.3$\,pp on AMC-23) that exceeds the magnitude of its
reported lift; single-seed claims at this scale are unreliable.
\textbf{(iii)~Negative.} On 10-language MGSM, all training conditions
converge to within $\pm 0.5$\,pp of the untrained base.

We initially hypothesized that \armC{}'s effect was a pure
reward-magnitude perturbation on the PPO clipping geometry, predicting
that the constant-bias ablation \armD{} would reproduce it. \armD{}
\emph{fails} to reproduce \armC{}'s AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8} result
($\armC{}{-}\armD{}{=}{+}5.58$\,pp, $95\%$ bootstrap CI
$[+1.13, +11.10]$, excluding $0$), demonstrating that $\R{5}$'s
contribution is content-specific rather than a pure reward-magnitude
perturbation. The most likely source of the load-bearing gradient
signal is the small fraction of training generations where the model
code-switches and $\R{5}\to 0$; a clean attribution of which non-EN
tokens drive the gradient is the natural follow-up. All code,
configurations, LoRA adapters, evaluation JSONs, and per-step reward
traces are released to enable independent replication.
