\begin{abstract}
\noindent\textbf{Background.}
Group Relative Policy Optimization
(GRPO)~\cite{deepseekmath_2024,deepseekR1_2025} drives recent advances in
mathematical reasoning for sub-3B language models, but a recent
reproducibility analysis~\cite{sober_look_2025} shows that small-model RL
gains often fail to survive multi-seed evaluation. The roles of
training-language choice and auxiliary reward components remain
understudied at the deployment-relevant single-GPU
LoRA~\cite{hu2022lora} scale.

\noindent\textbf{Methods.}
On the DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B base, we run a controlled multi-seed
GRPO study under a single A100 80\,GB LoRA constraint ($r{=}16$). Three
primary arms vary exactly one axis at a time: \armA{} (English data,
$R_1{+}R_2$), \armB{} (Vietnamese-translated data, identical rewards),
and \armC{} (English data plus a fastText-based~\cite{joulin2017fasttext}
language-consistency reward~$R_5$). Each arm is trained with three random
seeds. A three-seed constant-bias ablation \armD{} tests whether the
auxiliary-reward effect reduces to reward magnitude. We evaluate on
AMC-23, MATH-500, AIME-2024, and the 10-language MGSM
benchmark~\cite{mgsm_2022}.

\noindent\textbf{Results.}
We report three orthogonal findings.
\textbf{(i)~Positive.} On AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8} -- the hardest benchmark
in our suite -- \armC{} achieves the highest mean accuracy
($37.8 \pm 1.9$\,\%) and the only positive effect over the untrained base
($+4.4$\,pp), robust across three seeds.
\textbf{(ii)~Methodological.} \armA{} exhibits $\sigma{=}11.3$\,pp seed
variance on AMC-23 and consistently degrades AIME-2024 \texttt{pass@1}
(mean $-12.2$\,pp, $3/3$ seeds), confirming that single-seed claims at
sub-3B + LoRA scale are unreliable.
\textbf{(iii)~Negative.} On 10-language MGSM, all four training
conditions converge to within $\pm 0.5$\,pp of the untrained base mean,
indicating that training-language choice does not produce cross-lingual
transfer at this scale. The constant-bias ablation \armD{} fails to
reproduce \armC{}'s effect on AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8}
($\armC{}{-}\armD{}{=}{+}5.58$\,pp, $95\%$ bootstrap CI
$[+1.13, +11.10]$, excluding $0$), refuting a pure reward-magnitude
explanation.

\noindent\textbf{Conclusion.}
The auxiliary reward $R_5$ improves the hardest-benchmark capability while
preserving multilingual performance, but its contribution is
content-specific rather than a pure reward-magnitude perturbation. We
release all code, configurations, LoRA adapters, evaluation JSONs (with
full \texttt{responses[]} arrays), and per-step reward traces to support
independent replication.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Reinforcement learning from verifier signals has become the dominant
paradigm for improving mathematical reasoning in mid-sized language
models. GRPO~\cite{deepseekmath_2024,deepseekR1_2025} is the workhorse
of this paradigm: by sampling a group of candidate completions per
prompt and assigning advantages relative to the group mean, it removes
the need for a learned value head while preserving on-policy
PPO~\cite{schulman2017ppo}-style updates. Open-RS~\cite{openRS_2025}
shows that GRPO can lift a 1.5\,B distilled checkpoint to
$80$\,\% on AMC-23 with only 50 optimization steps, $7$\,K training
problems, and four NVIDIA A40 GPUs.

Two structural assumptions are baked into nearly every published GRPO
recipe at this scale:
(i)~the training corpus is monolingual English; and
(ii)~the reward sum is dominated by accuracy and format components.
The behaviour of GRPO when either assumption is perturbed has received
limited systematic attention at the sub-3\,B scale most relevant to
deployed systems and to independent researchers on commodity cloud
budgets. This paper supplies a multi-seed controlled study at that
scale, building on a single-seed preliminary
preprint~\cite{dang_v1_preprint_2026}.

\boxedclaim{%
\textbf{Main claim.} Multi-seed evaluation reveals three orthogonal
patterns. (i)~The language-consistency reward arm \armC{} achieves the
highest mean AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8} ($+4.4 \pm 1.9$\,pp over base across
three seeds). (ii)~Vanilla English GRPO \armA{} exhibits
$\sigma{=}11.3$\,pp seed variance on AMC-23 and consistently degrades
AIME-2024 \texttt{pass@1} by $-12.2 \pm 7.7$\,pp. (iii)~On 10-language
MGSM, all training conditions converge to within $\pm 0.5$\,pp of the
untrained base. A three-seed constant-bias control \armD{} fails to
reproduce \armC{}'s effect on AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8}
($\armC{}{-}\armD{}{=}{+}5.58$\,pp, $95\%$ bootstrap CI
$[+1.13, +11.10]$), refuting a pure reward-magnitude explanation.}

\paragraph{Why sub-3B and a single GPU?}
Sub-3B reasoning models are the deployment frontier for resource-constrained
applications. We deliberately target the hardware ceiling at which most
independent researchers can reproduce GRPO results: a single A100 80\,GB
GPU on commodity cloud infrastructure. Open-RS~\cite{openRS_2025} reports
$80$\,\% AMC-23 \texttt{maj@k} with $4{\times}$A40 full-parameter training
($192$\,GB total VRAM); we use LoRA $r{=}16$ as the natural fallback at
one-quarter VRAM and reach $57.5$\,\% \texttt{pass@1} (or $75.0$\,\%
\texttt{maj@4} on Open-RS's own released RS2 checkpoint, within $5$\,pp of
their headline), placing the residual gap on training capacity (LoRA vs
full-parameter) rather than the evaluation pipeline.

\paragraph{What differs from prior work.}
Open-RS~\cite{openRS_2025} does not vary training language or reward function.
\citet{xlingual_collapse_2025} report cross-lingual collapse at $3$\,B+ but
vary only the test language. M-Thinker~\cite{mthinker_2025} works at sub-3\,B
but focuses on Chinese-only multilingual training. Our three-arm
comparison (\armA{}~EN, \armB{}~VI, \armC{}~EN${+}R_5$) is, to our
knowledge, the first controlled study at this scale where exactly one
axis (language or reward) is changed at a time on a single base model and
budget.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep0pt
\item Multi-seed empirical evidence (3 seeds $\times$ 4 arms) that
single-seed GRPO results at sub-3\,B + LoRA scale are unreliable
($\sigma{=}11.3$\,pp on AMC-23 for vanilla English training).
\item Documentation of \armC{}'s positive effect on the hardest
benchmark: $+4.4 \pm 1.9$\,pp on AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8} over the
untrained base, robust across three seeds.
\item A three-seed constant-bias ablation (\armD{}) that fails to
reproduce \armC{}'s AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8} result
($\armC{}{-}\armD{}{=}{+}5.58$\,pp, $95\%$ bootstrap CI
$[+1.13, +11.10]$, excluding $0$), demonstrating that $R_5$'s
contribution is content-specific rather than a pure reward-magnitude
perturbation.
\item A 10-language MGSM evaluation showing that the GRPO recipe at
this scale \emph{preserves but does not improve} multilingual capability
regardless of training-language choice.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{GRPO for small reasoning models}
DeepSeekMath~\cite{deepseekmath_2024} introduced GRPO, replacing PPO's
learned value head with a group-relative baseline. DeepSeek-R1~\cite{deepseekR1_2025}
scaled this technique and produced distilled checkpoints (including the
1.5\,B model used here) that have become standard sub-3\,B baselines.
Open-RS~\cite{openRS_2025} explored which hyperparameters lift a
distilled 1.5\,B to $80$\,\% on AMC-23 within 50 steps; we adopt their
RS2 recipe verbatim for \armA{}. Variance- and length-normalized
variants -- Dr.GRPO~\cite{drgrpo_2025} and DAPO~\cite{dapo_2025} --
are orthogonal to the axes we vary.

\subsection{Cross-lingual mathematical reasoning}
Multilingual mathematical reasoning has been studied primarily at
$7$\,B and above. LinguaLIFT~\cite{lingualift_2024} proposes a two-stage
instruction tuning framework. MAPO~\cite{mapo_2024} and
MathOctopus~\cite{mathoctopus_2024} fine-tune open models for
multilingual mathematical reasoning at 7\,B scale.
\citet{xreasoning_test_time_2025} examines inference-time multilingual
reasoning via test-time scaling. The multilingual GSM
benchmark~\cite{mgsm_2022} is the de-facto evaluation harness;
OpenMathInstruct-2~\cite{openmathinstruct2_2024} supplies English-only
training data. Cross-lingual collapse~\cite{xlingual_collapse_2025} is
the closest concurrent work, observing language drift during GRPO at
$\geq 3$\,B; our setting differs in three respects: (i)~sub-3\,B with a
single-GPU LoRA budget where prior baselines underperform their reported
numbers; (ii)~a controlled \emph{training-language} ablation in addition
to test-language variation; and (iii)~explicit reward intervention as a
treatment. M-Thinker~\cite{mthinker_2025} is the closest scale-matched
work but evaluates only on Chinese.

\subsection{Auxiliary rewards and reward shaping}
Reward shaping is well-studied in classical RL.
\citet{ng1999policy} proves that potential-based shaping preserves the
optimal policy, while \citet{skalse2022rewardhacking} characterize how
mis-specified auxiliary rewards can induce reward hacking.
\citet{zheng_intrinsic_2018} study learned intrinsic rewards in policy
gradients and provide constant-bias baselines that are conceptually
analogous to our \armD{} ablation. In language-model fine-tuning,
auxiliary rewards are typically interpreted as injecting content-level
signal into the policy gradient. To our knowledge, no prior work
examines what happens when an auxiliary reward fires nearly uniformly
across training samples and therefore carries minimal content
information. The \armC{} arm provides a natural setting to test this
regime; the \armD{} ablation (Section~\ref{sec:a4_ablation})
operationalizes the prediction.

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:preliminaries}

We restate the relevant pieces of the GRPO objective to fix notation
for the mechanistic discussion in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\paragraph{GRPO objective.}
Let $\pi_\theta$ denote the current policy and
$\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ the behaviour policy at the start of the
optimization step. For each prompt $\mathbf{p}$, GRPO samples
$G$ completions $\{\mathbf{c}^{(1)},\ldots,\mathbf{c}^{(G)}\}$ and
computes a scalar reward
$R(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{c}^{(g)})\in\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ per completion.
The group-relative advantage is
\begin{equation}
A^{(g)} = \frac{R(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{c}^{(g)}) - \bar{R}_{\mathbf{p}}}%
              {\sigma_{R,\mathbf{p}} + \varepsilon},
\label{eq:advantage}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{R}_{\mathbf{p}}$ and $\sigma_{R,\mathbf{p}}$ are the
within-group mean and standard deviation. With per-token importance
ratios $\rho_t^{(g)}$ clipped via the standard PPO trust
region~\cite{schulman2017ppo}, the GRPO loss is
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{GRPO}}(\theta) &=
\E_{\mathbf{p}}\Big[\sum_g \sum_t \min\!\big(\rho_t^{(g)} A^{(g)}, \nonumber\\
& \quad \mathrm{clip}(\rho_t^{(g)},1{-}\epsilon,1{+}\epsilon) A^{(g)}\big)\Big] \nonumber\\
& \quad - \beta_{\text{KL}}\,\KL(\pi_\theta\,\|\,\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}).
\label{eq:grpo}
\end{align}

\paragraph{Advantage invariance under additive offsets.}
With multiple reward components $\R{1},\ldots,\R{K}$ and weights $w_k$,
the per-completion reward is
$R(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{c}) = \sum_k w_k \R{k}(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{c})$.
For any constant $b\in\mathbb{R}$,
\begin{equation}
A^{(g)}\!\left[R + b\right]
= \frac{(R + b) - (\bar{R}+b)}{\sigma_R + \varepsilon}
= A^{(g)}\!\left[R\right],
\label{eq:advantage-invariance}
\end{equation}
i.e., adding a constant offset to every completion's reward leaves the
advantage unchanged. This is the textbook reason ``trivially-satisfied''
auxiliary components are believed inert. Equation~\eqref{eq:advantage-invariance}
ignores the rest of the optimization machinery -- in particular the
clipping ratio $\rho_t^{(g)}$ and the KL penalty -- which depend on
\emph{realized rewards}, not advantages, and are therefore \emph{not}
invariant to additive offsets. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} returns to
this point.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Base model and training pipeline}
All arms start from the publicly-released
\path{deepseek-ai/DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B}
checkpoint~\cite{deepseekR1_2025}. We do \emph{not} use a separate SFT
stage (matching Open-RS): training is GRPO directly from the distilled
base. Each cell is a single (arm, seed) pair, producing one LoRA adapter
at step~50.

\subsection{Reward functions}
\label{sec:rewards}
All arms share two rewards (Open-RS RS2 setup):
\begin{itemize}\itemsep0pt
\item $\R{1}$~\textbf{correctness}: indicator of
Math-Verify~\cite{mathverify_2024} equivalence between the extracted
answer $\hat{y}$ and the gold $y^\star$. Extraction priority:
\texttt{<answer>} tag, then \texttt{\textbackslash boxed\{\}}, then
last-number fallback.
\item $\R{2}$~\textbf{format}: indicator that the completion matches a
regex requiring both \texttt{<think>...</think>} and
\texttt{<answer>...</answer>} blocks, in that order; matches the
training system prompt template.
\end{itemize}
Arm \armC{} adds the language-consistency reward $\R{5}$:
\begin{equation}
\R{5}(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{c}) =
\begin{cases}
0 & \text{if } |\mathbf{c}| < 10,\\[2pt]
\mathbf{1}[\mathrm{lang}(\mathbf{c}) = \mathrm{lang}(\mathbf{p})]
                & \text{otherwise,}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:r5}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{lang}(\cdot)$ is the fastText~\cite{joulin2017fasttext}
\texttt{lid.176.bin} language identifier, and the $|\mathbf{c}|{<}10$
guard accounts for fastText's unreliability on very short strings. With
English training data the model rarely code-switches; thus
$\R{5}\approx 1$ for almost every training generation, and $\R{5}$
provides effectively zero content-discrimination signal in this regime.
We discuss the implication in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Three primary arms}
\begin{description}\itemsep0pt
\item[\armA{} (English-only)] training data \texttt{knoveleng/open-rs}
(7\,K English problems, no SFT); rewards $\R{1}+\R{2}$.
\item[\armB{} (Vietnamese-translated)] training data
\path{5CD-AI/Vietnamese-MetaMathQA-40K-gg-translated} (5{,}203 records
extracted from a 7\,K random subset by retaining only those whose
Vietnamese response contains the answer marker
``\DJ\'ap \'an l\`a''); rewards $\R{1}+\R{2}$.
\item[\armC{} (English with $\R{5}$)] same training data as \armA{};
rewards $\R{1}+\R{2}+\R{5}$ with $\R{5}$'s
\texttt{no\_penalty\_for\_en} flag disabled so it can fire on every
prompt.
\end{description}
A fourth ablation arm \armD{} replaces $\R{5}$ with a deterministic
constant reward of $1.0$; we describe it together with the mechanism
hypothesis in Section~\ref{sec:a4_ablation}.

\subsection{Compute and hyperparameters}
Hardware: $1\times$ NVIDIA A100-SXM4-80\,GB on commodity cloud
infrastructure. LoRA~\cite{hu2022lora}: $r{=}16$, $\alpha{=}32$,
dropout~$0.05$, \texttt{bias=none}, target modules
$\{q,k,v,o\}\_\mathrm{proj}$. GRPO: $G{=}6$,
\texttt{max\_completion\_length}$=3584$, \texttt{temperature}$=0.7$,
$\beta_{\text{KL}}=0.04$, learning rate $10^{-6}$, scheduler
\texttt{cosine\_with\_min\_lr} (\texttt{min\_lr\_rate}$=0.1$,
warmup $0.1$). Effective batch is 96 prompts (per-device 6 $\times$
gradient accumulation 16), \texttt{bf16}, flash-attention~2, gradient
checkpointing. Training uses TRL~\cite{trl_2025} 0.15.2 with
in-process vLLM~\cite{kwon2023vllm} 0.7.2 rollout
(\texttt{gpu\_memory\_utilization}$=0.25$, leaving $\sim 60$\,GB for
training). We save at step 50 (Open-RS RS2 peak).

\subsection{Evaluation}
We use the Open-RS \texttt{lighteval} \texttt{MATH\_QUERY\_TEMPLATE}
verbatim (no system prompt; evaluation template embedded in the user
message), with greedy decoding ($T{=}0$), \texttt{max\_tokens}$=8192$,
and no early stop. Aligning to this template closes the AIME-2024 gap
to Open-RS-reported numbers from $-10$\,pp to $-2.1$\,pp. Benchmarks:
AMC-23~\cite{amc23_dataset} (40 problems, \texttt{pass@1} and
\texttt{maj@4}), MATH-500~\cite{lightman2024verify} (500,
\texttt{pass@1}), and AIME-2024~\cite{aime2024_dataset} (30,
\texttt{pass@1} plus \texttt{maj@8} with seeds 42--49), and
MGSM~\cite{mgsm_2022} (250 problems each in 10 languages,
\texttt{pass@1}).

\subsection{Finding 1: English-only GRPO causes benchmark-specific overfit}
\label{sec:finding1}
Averaged over three seeds, \armA{} gains $+6.7 \pm 11.3$\,pp on AMC-23
and loses $-12.2 \pm 7.7$\,pp on AIME-2024 \texttt{pass@1} relative to
base (Table~\ref{tab:delta_v2}). The AMC-23 lift is not statistically
distinguishable from zero given the variance, but the AIME-2024
degradation is robust: $3/3$ seeds fall below base. AIME-2024
\texttt{maj@8} (averaged over eight stochastic samples) shows a smaller
mean drop of $-1.1 \pm 6.9$\,pp, suggesting that the underlying
capability is partially preserved in the sample distribution but greedy
decoding under \armA{}'s policy lands on shallower trajectories more
often.

\subsection{Finding 2: Both \armB{} and \armC{} reduce seed variance}
\label{sec:finding2}
Across three seeds, \armA{} exhibits $\sigma{=}11.3$\,pp on AMC-23 and
$\sigma{=}6.9$\,pp on AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8}. Both \armB{} and
\armC{} reduce this variance substantially: $\sigma{=}5.2$ and
$6.6$\,pp respectively on AMC-23, and $\sigma{=}1.9$\,pp for both on
AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8} (Figure~\ref{fig:variance_ratios}). The mean
lifts on AMC-23 are statistically indistinguishable across arms
($56.7$, $56.7$, $57.5$\,\%); the differentiation appears in
\emph{reproducibility} rather than \emph{mean performance}.

\subsection{Finding 3: Lang-consistency reward is best on AIME-2024 maj@8}
\label{sec:finding3}
\armC{} achieves the highest mean AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8}
($37.8 \pm 1.9$\,\%) and is the only arm with a positive $\Delta$ over
base ($+4.4$\,pp; Figure~\ref{fig:aime_focus}). The effect is robust:
all three seeds produce $36.7$\,\% or higher. The mean reward in
\armC{} shifts from $\E[\R{1}{+}\R{2}]\approx 0.2$ in \armA{} to
$\E[\R{1}{+}\R{2}{+}\R{5}]\approx 1.2$, because $\R{5}$ fires
near-uniformly at $1.0$ on English training data. By the
advantage-invariance identity~\eqref{eq:advantage-invariance}, this
offset is invisible to the within-group advantage; we test whether it
is invisible to the rest of the optimization machinery via the
constant-bias ablation \armD{} (Section~\ref{sec:a4_ablation}).

\subsection{MGSM multilingual evaluation}
\label{sec:mgsm}
We evaluate all training arms plus the untrained base on the
10-language MGSM benchmark (250 problems per language). The MGSM
evaluation has limited seed coverage relative to the
AMC-23/MATH-500/AIME-2024 suite: the untrained base and \armD{} report
a single seed (\texttt{42}); \armA{}, \armB{}, \armC{} report the mean
of two seeds (\texttt{123} and \texttt{7}). Results in
Table~\ref{tab:mgsm} show that all four training conditions converge
to within $\pm 0.5$\,pp of the untrained base mean
($\base{}$ $47.3$\,\%; \armA{} $47.2$, \armB{} $46.9$, \armC{} $46.8$,
\armD{} $47.5$). Per-language $\Delta$s remain within seed variance on
every language. Training-language choice (English vs Vietnamese) does
\emph{not} produce cross-lingual transfer at this scale; the GRPO
recipe preserves rather than improves multilingual capability. A full
three-seed MGSM evaluation is left to future work due to the
additional $\approx 6$ A100-hours per run.

\section{Constant-Bias Ablation (\armD{})}
\label{sec:a4_ablation}

To test whether \armC{}'s effect reduces to a pure reward-magnitude
perturbation, we define a fourth arm \armD{} that replaces $\R{5}$ with
a deterministic constant reward of $1.0$:
$R_{\armD{}}(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{c}) = \R{1}+\R{2}+\R{\text{const}}$,
$\R{\text{const}}\equiv 1.0$. If the magnitude-only hypothesis is
correct, \armD{} should reproduce \armC{}'s regularization signature on
AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8} within seed variance. We train three seeds
(\texttt{42, 123, 7}) using the same protocol as \armA{} and \armC{}.

\paragraph{Results.} Three-seed mean$\pm\sigma$:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep0pt
\item AMC-23 \texttt{pass@1}: \armD{} $=52.5\pm 0.0$\,\% vs \armC{}
$57.5\pm 6.6$\,\% (\armD{} below \armC{}'s $1\sigma$ band).
\item AMC-23 \texttt{maj@4}: \armD{} $=70.0\pm 2.5$\,\% vs \armC{}
$68.3\pm 3.8$\,\% (overlap within $1\sigma$).
\item MATH-500: \armD{} $=61.2\pm 0.9$\,\% vs \armC{} $60.7\pm 0.6$\,\%
(overlap).
\item AIME-2024 \texttt{pass@1}: \armD{} $=24.4\pm 1.9$\,\% vs \armC{}
$21.1\pm 1.9$\,\% (\armD{} slightly above \armC{}, within sampling
noise).
\item AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8}: \armD{} $=32.2\pm 5.1$\,\% vs \armC{}
$37.8\pm 1.9$\,\% ($\armC{}-\armD{}=+5.58$\,pp; $95\%$ subject-bootstrap
CI $[+1.13, +11.10]$ over $10{,}000$ resamples, excluding $0$).
\end{itemize}

\armD{} \emph{does not} reproduce \armC{}'s AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8}
result. \armD{} itself is indistinguishable from base on this benchmark
($\Delta = -1.08 \pm 5.07$\,pp; CI includes $0$): the constant-bias
control does not move the model on the hardest benchmark. The
mechanism behind $\R{5}$'s effect is therefore \emph{content-specific,
not reward-magnitude-based}; the small fraction of training generations
where the model code-switches and $\R{5} \to 0$ apparently provides
the load-bearing gradient signal, which a content-free constant offset
cannot supply.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why does \armC{} succeed despite $\R{5}$ being trivially
satisfied?}
The standard view is that an auxiliary reward must inject useful
content signal. $\R{5}$ fires near-uniformly at $1.0$ on \armC{}'s
English training data -- a regime where the simple ``content signal''
interpretation predicts no effect. We initially hypothesized a pure
reward-magnitude mechanism: shifting $\E[R]$ upward by a constant
offset is invisible to the within-group advantage~\eqref{eq:advantage-invariance}
but should still affect the rest of the optimization machinery
(PPO clipping ratios $\rho_t^{(g)}$, KL trust-region width, gradient
norms). The constant-bias ablation \armD{} was designed to test this
hypothesis.

\paragraph{\armD{} refutes the magnitude-only hypothesis.}
\armD{} matches the direction of \armC{}'s effects on AMC-23 and
MATH-500 ($\pm$ within seed variance) but does \emph{not} reproduce
\armC{}'s AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8} result (\armD{}~$=32.2 \pm 5.1$\,\%
vs \armC{}~$=37.8 \pm 1.9$\,\%; $95\%$ CI $[+1.13, +11.10]$ on the
difference, excluding $0$). \armD{} is statistically
indistinguishable from base on this benchmark
($\Delta = -1.08 \pm 5.07$\,pp; CI includes $0$). $\R{5}$'s
contribution is therefore content-specific, not a pure
reward-magnitude perturbation. The most likely remaining source of
content signal is the rare fraction of training generations where the
model code-switches and $\R{5} \to 0$, providing scarce but
informative gradient signal that a content-free constant offset cannot
supply. A clean attribution of which non-EN tokens drive the gradient
signal would require gradient-tracing on individual generations,
which we leave to future work.

\paragraph{Cross-lingual transfer is null at this scale.}
Practitioners might expect that training on Vietnamese-translated math
would transfer to multilingual benchmarks. The MGSM 10-language
evaluation in Section~\ref{sec:mgsm} refutes this hope: all four
training conditions plus base converge to within $\pm 0.5$\,pp of each
other on the 10-language mean. The GRPO recipe at sub-3\,B + LoRA
scale preserves but does not improve multilingual reasoning
capability, regardless of training-language choice.

\paragraph{Practical implication.}
For low-resource settings where English math data is abundant but the
target is robustness rather than language coverage, our results suggest
keeping the original English data and adding an auxiliary reward whose
content relevance is secondary -- much cheaper than translating the
training corpus -- as a low-cost regularization handle. The exact
gradient mechanism behind this regularization is the natural follow-up
question.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Three-seed sample (all four arms).}
Each of \armA{}, \armB{}, \armC{}, and \armD{} reports three random
seeds (\texttt{42, 123, 7}). The headline finding on AIME-2024
\texttt{maj@8} ($\sigma{=}1.9$\,pp across three \armC{} seeds;
$\sigma{=}5.07$\,pp across three \armD{} seeds) is robust under this
sample size, and the $\armC{}{-}\armD{}$ contrast is supported by a
$95\%$ bootstrap CI of $[+1.13, +11.10]$ that excludes~$0$ ($10{,}000$
resamples, subject bootstrap; see Section~\ref{sec:a4_ablation}). $n{=}3$
is the standard for sub-3\,B GRPO replication studies, but a larger
seed sample would tighten the intervals further.

\paragraph{LoRA-only training.}
Open-RS used full-parameter on $4\times$A40; we use LoRA $r{=}16$ on
$1\times$A100 to fit at the same effective batch size of 96. Our
findings about \emph{relative} arm comparisons may interact with LoRA's
limited update capacity in unknown ways. The apparent $22.5$\,pp
``gap'' to Open-RS-reported $80$\,\% AMC-23 dissolves under
like-for-like \texttt{maj@4} comparison on Open-RS's own public
checkpoint (see ``Evaluation pipeline gap'' below).

\paragraph{\armB{} dataset is not a faithful translation of \armA{}.}
\armB{} uses \path{5CD-AI/Vietnamese-MetaMathQA-40K-gg-translated}, a
Google-translated MetaMathQA, not a translation of the same Open-RS
subset used in \armA{}. Confounds between dataset content and
language make Finding~2 a language-\emph{plus}-distribution effect
rather than a clean language-only effect.

\paragraph{Evaluation pipeline gap explained by metric mismatch.}
Open-RS's reported $80$\,\% AMC-23 headline for the RS2 checkpoint is
majority-vote (\texttt{maj@k}), not greedy \texttt{pass@1}. Evaluating
the public \texttt{knoveleng/Open-RS2} checkpoint with our pipeline
gives \texttt{pass@1}$=52.5$\,\% (within sampling variance of our
$57.5$\,\% LoRA best arm) and \texttt{maj@4}$=75.0$\,\% -- only
$5$\,pp below Open-RS's $80$\,\% headline. The base
\texttt{DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B} gives \texttt{pass@1}$=50.0$\,\%
(matching our $50.0$\,\% base claim) and \texttt{maj@4}$=70.0$\,\%
(vs Open-RS-reported $62.9$\,\%, $+7$\,pp above). The original
$12.9$\,pp gap was a metric-mismatch artifact, not a pipeline bug.

\paragraph{\texttt{maj@4} on AMC-23 -- bug found and fixed.}
The pre-revision \texttt{maj@4}$=0$ numbers across all arms were caused
by two bugs: (i)~\texttt{eval.yaml} omitted a \texttt{temperature\_maj}
field, causing all four majority samples to be deterministic and
identical; and (ii)~\texttt{extract\_prediction()} did not strip
whitespace, causing valid answers to fail string matching. Both fixes
are in the v2 commit; the post-fix \texttt{maj@4} numbers appear in
Table~\ref{tab:main_v2}.

\paragraph{Single base model and scale.}
All experiments use
\path{deepseek-ai/DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B}.
Generalization to Qwen2.5-Instruct, larger 3\,B+ checkpoints, or
non-distilled bases remains untested in this manuscript.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We provided a multi-seed empirical study of GRPO post-training at
sub-3\,B scale under a single-GPU LoRA constraint, with three
orthogonal findings.
\textbf{(i)~Positive.} The language-consistency reward arm \armC{}
achieves the highest mean AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8} ($37.8 \pm 1.9$\,\%),
exceeding the untrained base by $+4.4$\,pp and robust across three
seeds.
\textbf{(ii)~Methodological.} Vanilla English GRPO \armA{} exhibits seed
variance ($\sigma{=}11.3$\,pp on AMC-23, $\sigma{=}7.7$\,pp on
AIME-2024 \texttt{pass@1}) that exceeds the magnitude of its reported
lift; single-seed claims at this scale are unreliable.
\textbf{(iii)~Negative.} On 10-language MGSM, all training conditions
converge to within $\pm 0.5$\,pp of the untrained base mean.
Training-language choice does not produce cross-lingual transfer at
this scale.
A three-seed constant-bias ablation \armD{} \emph{fails} to reproduce
\armC{}'s effect on AIME-2024 \texttt{maj@8}
($\armC{}{-}\armD{}{=}{+}5.58$\,pp, $95\%$ bootstrap CI
$[+1.13, +11.10]$, excluding $0$), demonstrating that $\R{5}$'s
contribution is content-specific rather than a pure reward-magnitude
perturbation. We release all code, configurations, LoRA adapters,
evaluation JSONs, and per-step reward traces to support replication.
